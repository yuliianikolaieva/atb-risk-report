Why it matters

  • With ~€12M annual GMV from 2027, even partial materialisation of the unlimited / one-sided liability clauses (e.g. up to 100% revenue recovery, 20% VAT + penalties) represents a material exposure relative to the revenue earned.

Financial exposure — €, indicative

Estimated monetary losses if the penalty / compensation clauses are triggered, under Ukrainian tax law, benchmarked against GMV.

First, in plain terms: what "take rate" means

  • Take rate is the commission Bolt earns on ATB's sales, as a % of GMV. The actual Bolt–ATB commission = 3.33% (excl. VAT).
  • It matters because almost every penalty and tax compensation is calculated on the "service value" — i.e. Bolt's fees / revenue (~3.33% of GMV), not on the whole €12M GMV (grocery sales, not Bolt's income).
  • So Bolt's revenue from ATB is small (~€0.4M in 2027), and all amounts below are computed at the 3.33% commission.

Bottom line — does it exceed our GMV?

  • No — and not even close to GMV. Even the worst legal scenario (~€576k in 2027, all penalties + budget recovery stacking) is only ~4.8% of GMV (€12M), because Bolt's commission is small (3.33%) and penalties are based on it, not on grocery sales.
  • But it can exceed Bolt's revenue from ATB. Worst case ≈ ~144% of the fees Bolt earns from this client (~€400k in 2027) — i.e. in that scenario the deal becomes loss-making and we'd pay out more than we earned.
  • Cumulative worst case 2026 + 2027 ≈ €0.77M.
  • Key mitigant: the largest component (budget recovery + transfer of tax liability) is likely legally unenforceable per the Supreme Court ruling of 6 Feb 2026 (No. 904/2953/24). Realistically enforceable exposure is closer to Scenario ① (contractual fixed penalties), in the €10k–50k/yr range.

These are indicative estimates to size the risk for discussion, not legal or tax advice. Computed at the 3.33% commission (excl. VAT); amounts depend on invoice volumes and the share of invoices actually challenged.

Executive summary

The Additional Agreement shifts almost all tax, criminal-procedure and registration risk onto Bolt as Contractor, with broad, one-sided liability and several penalties for events Bolt does not fully control.

What management needs to decide

  • Unlimited, one-sided liability. Bolt would compensate the Customer for tax losses, penalties and even budget recovery — in some cases up to 100% of the contract revenue — frequently regardless of proven fault.
  • Liability for events outside Bolt's control (blocked/suspended tax-invoice registration, third-party fictitious counterparties, criminal proceedings without a verdict).
  • Cash-flow exposure. 20% of every payment is deferred until tax-invoice registration, and the Customer may freeze payments at its discretion.
  • Operational burden & fixed penalties (UAH 10k / 20k / 100k) for administrative breaches and shortened registration deadlines.
  • Key mitigant (Baker McKenzie): the contractual transfer of tax / public-law liability is likely legally unenforceable — per the Supreme Court ruling of 6 Feb 2026 (case No. 904/2953/24), such provisions "do not give rise to the legal consequences they are intended to produce". Several penalties are assessed as punitive and inconsistent with this position.
  • Recommendation: negotiate caps on liability, a fault requirement, removal of liability for state-side / counterparty actions outside Bolt's control, and realistic deadlines — see auditor comments per clause below.
